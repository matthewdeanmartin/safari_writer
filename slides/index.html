Safari Writer: product progress, remaining work, and go-to-market path

A retro-styled writing environment built with Python + Textual, combining editor, proofreader, mail merge, print/export, and Safari DOS file management.

Current state

Core product is working. The biggest job now is polish, completion, and positioning.

Pitch framing

We are selling disciplined progress on a differentiated niche product, not vapor.

Speaker notes

  • Open by framing this as an honest operating review, not a hype deck.
  • Say the repo shows a real product with meaningful shipped functionality and a clear roadmap.
  • Set expectation that this deck distinguishes what is done, what is partly done, and what is still aspirational.

Executive summary

  • Product exists today: editor, formatting, proofreader, mail merge, print/export, and Safari DOS all have real implementation.
  • Validation signal: current test suite passes at 586 tests from a fresh make test run.
  • Spec maturity: most core specs are largely implemented; the major untouched area is the AI chat spec.
  • Best near-term use of capital: finish polish and workflow gaps before expanding into ambitious AI features.
  • Market story: retro UX + practical writing workflows + terminal-native simplicity gives us a clear niche.
  • Recommendation: fund a focused push to turn a promising 0.1 product into a reliable v1 wedge.

Speaker notes

  • Lead with credibility: we are not pre-product.
  • Use the 586-test point as evidence that this is a living codebase, not just a spec folder.
  • Frame the investment case around finishing and packaging a strong niche product before chasing every idea on the roadmap.

What Safari Writer is building

Product thesis

  • Modern spiritual successor to AtariWriter with a Textual terminal UI.
  • "What you see is what you mean" editor model: formatting is explicit and visible.
  • Designed for focused writing, editing, light publishing, and structured document workflows.

Target users

  • Retro-computing enthusiasts who want a real tool, not just an emulator.
  • Writers and technical users who prefer keyboard-first, low-distraction interfaces.
  • Developers and documentation teams who benefit from plain-text and syntax-aware workflows.

Evidence base: README, pyproject, TODOs, and spec set in spec\*.md.

Speaker notes

  • Describe Safari Writer as a product category hybrid: part editor, part retro experience, part document workflow tool.
  • Stress that the retro angle is the hook, but utility is the reason users stay.
  • Make clear we are not trying to replace every word processor; we are winning a narrower but defensible segment.

What is not done yet

High-value completion work

  • Mid-document global format overrides
  • Manual dictionary creation in proofreader
  • Double-column rendering in preview and PostScript
  • Safari DOS conflict-resolution and progress flows

Still-open product gaps

  • Undo remains an obvious UX gap called out in BUGS.md.
  • Some Safari DOS list navigation and filtering features remain open.
  • Cross-platform polish and edge-case handling still need concentrated effort.
  • The ELIZA-style AI chat spec exists, but implementation has not started.

The strongest investor story is "finish and harden the product" before "add every future concept."

Speaker notes

  • Be direct here; credibility goes up when we name the missing pieces ourselves.
  • Separate practical completion work from larger strategic bets like AI chat.
  • Call out undo because even if the retro original lacked it, modern users will expect it.

Challenges that remain

Product polish

Undo, save flows, prompts, and edge-case UX still need tightening to meet modern expectations.

Rendering complexity

Preview, Markdown, and PostScript outputs must stay aligned as formatting features expand.

Cross-platform behavior

Windows terminal quirks and file-system differences make QA and support more demanding.

Scope discipline

There is a risk of spreading effort across AI, DOS polish, and editorial features all at once.

Bottom line: the project's main risk is not vision failure; it is finishing quality-critical details fast enough.

Speaker notes

  • Investors want to hear that we know the difference between feature count and product readiness.
  • Emphasize that the remaining risk profile is execution-heavy, not science-fiction-heavy.
  • That is generally a healthier risk shape for a small product at this stage.

Why this product can win

  • Differentiated UX: retro identity is memorable and marketable.
  • Practical utility: proofreader, mail merge, and export make it more than nostalgia.
  • Terminal-native: fits technical and keyboard-first audiences well.
  • Focused positioning: it does not need to beat Microsoft Word at everything.
  • Spec discipline: unusually detailed specs reduce product drift.
  • Local-first story: offline, lightweight, and installable with simple Python tooling.

Speaker notes

  • Frame the moat as taste + workflow + audience fit, not just raw technology.
  • Local-first and terminal-native matter more than they appear because they align with the product's ideal community.
  • Explain that this is a strong niche wedge that can later expand outward.

What sort of LLM bots we should hire next

1. QA triage bot

  • Reads specs, TODOs, and BUGS
  • Turns gaps into reproducible test cases
  • Best ROI for finishing work cleanly

2. Textual UX bot

  • Specialist in keyboard flows and TUI ergonomics
  • Focus on prompts, menus, layout, and affordances
  • Key for Safari DOS and editor polish

3. Print/rendering bot

  • Owns preview / Markdown / PostScript alignment
  • Reduces regressions in layout logic
  • Supports higher-confidence output features

4. Documentation / demo bot

  • Creates docs, scripts, walkthroughs, and release notes
  • Improves onboarding and investor/customer demos
  • Useful immediately

5. Growth copy bot

  • Writes landing pages, launch posts, and email sequences
  • Tunes positioning by audience segment
  • Supports market discovery without huge agency spend

Wait on this one

  • Do not lead with an "AI chat bot builder" bot yet.
  • The AI chat spec is rich, but it is a second-stage investment.
  • Finish product readiness first, then expand intelligently.

Speaker notes

  • Present these as staffing archetypes, not literal headcount commitments.
  • The message is that AI help should accelerate finishing and go-to-market, not distract the roadmap.
  • Investors will usually like the discipline of delaying the flashy AI feature until the foundation is stronger.

Market fit: where the wedge is

Best initial segments

  • Retro-computing and digital minimalism communities
  • Technical writers and developer-documentation users
  • Writers who want a local-first, distraction-light environment

Good reasons they may buy in

  • Distinctive writing experience they can feel immediately
  • Practical export and proofreading capabilities
  • No cloud dependency and a straightforward install path
Best thesis: Safari Writer is not mass-market office software; it is premium-fit software for users who care about focused writing and characterful tools.

Speaker notes

  • Do not oversell TAM here; a believable niche story is stronger than a fake giant-market claim.
  • Highlight that niche products can still become durable businesses when the audience is passionate and well-served.
  • Explain that technical writers are especially interesting because they already tolerate terminal tools.

Competition and positioning
